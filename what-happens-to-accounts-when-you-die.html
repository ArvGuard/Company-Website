What Actually Happens to Your Accounts When You Die (Nobody Talks About This)

Your will covers your assets. It doesn't cover your passwords. Here's what really happens to your digital accounts when you're gone — and why the legal system isn't set up to help your family the way you think it is.

March 10, 2026
7 min read
ArvGuard

My uncle passed away three years ago. He was organized — had a will, had a lawyer, had everything in what his family thought was order. What they didn't have was a password to a single account. Not his bank. Not his email. Not the investment account that turned out to have a significant balance. Not the cloud storage with years of photos his kids desperately wanted.

His wife spent the better part of a year fighting with customer service departments, probate courts, and identity verification systems that were designed — in every way — to keep unauthorized people out. Even his own family. Even his widow. It was a nightmare that had nothing to do with grief and everything to do with the gap between how we actually live our lives now and how estate law was written in a different era.

I think about that a lot.

The honest answer is: it depends, and not in a good way

The fate of your digital accounts after death isn't governed by any single law. It's a patchwork of terms of service agreements, state statutes that vary wildly, federal laws written before smartphones existed, and the individual policies of hundreds of companies — none of which are required to coordinate.

Your Gmail account is covered by Google's policies, which allow next-of-kin to request account access but don't guarantee it. Your Apple ID is governed by iCloud terms that explicitly state your account cannot be transferred. Your Fidelity brokerage account is a financial asset with legal protections — but your login credentials are a completely separate thing from the account ownership itself. These two things, the asset and the access, don't automatically go together.

That's the part nobody talks about. You can be the legal heir to a financial account and still have no idea how to log into it.

What happens in practice

Here's what actually plays out when someone dies and their family tries to get access to accounts:

For financial accounts — banks, brokerages, retirement accounts — there are legal pathways. Letters testamentary, death certificates, court orders. It's a bureaucratic slog but it works eventually. Most families get there in weeks or months. They often lose interest accrued in the meantime. They sometimes incur legal fees. But they get there.

For everything else, it's a different story.

Email is the worst. Email is the skeleton key to every other account — password resets, two-factor authentication, identity verification all flow through it. And email providers are notoriously difficult about granting access to next-of-kin. Google has an "Inactive Account Manager" feature that lets you set a plan for your account. Most people have never heard of it and haven't touched it.

Social media accounts can often be memorialized — converted to a tribute page — but not accessed. The content — messages, photos, shared memories — may be accessible in some cases and completely locked away in others. It often depends on whether you requested a data download before you died. Which, obviously, you didn't.

Subscriptions keep charging. Netflix, Spotify, Amazon, a dozen other services — they don't stop billing because you died. Until someone cancels them, they'll keep drawing from whatever payment method is on file. Families sometimes discover active subscriptions months later.

Crypto and digital assets are their own special category of complicated. If you have cryptocurrency and the only key is in your head, it's gone. Full stop. There's no customer service department that can recover a private key.

The gap in existing solutions

Traditional estate planning — wills, trusts, power of attorney — was designed for a world where your assets were physical things. Real estate. Bank accounts you accessed in person. Objects you owned. The legal infrastructure around this is mature and genuinely works.

But it doesn't transfer cleanly to digital life. A will can state who inherits your assets. It cannot store your passwords. It cannot update itself when you change your Netflix login. It cannot specify different instructions for different types of accounts — delete my search history, preserve my photos, give my daughter access to my playlists.

Password managers solve part of this. If you use one and someone has access to it, that's a significant step forward. But most password managers aren't designed for death. They're designed for you. There's no native mechanism for "in the event I don't log in for 60 days, transfer access to this person." There's no way to attach instructions. There's no organized handoff process.

What most people do (which is nothing)

The pattern I keep seeing is that most people know this is a problem, feel vaguely anxious about it, and do nothing. Because the solution seems like it would require a lot of effort upfront. Or because thinking about your own death is uncomfortable. Or because it feels like something you'll handle "later" — which is what everyone says about estate planning in general until later suddenly becomes now.

The other thing I hear a lot is "my spouse knows my passwords." And that's something. It's genuinely better than nothing. But it's also fragile in ways people don't think about. It assumes you die alone. It doesn't help your kids if you and your spouse are both gone. It doesn't have instructions attached. It doesn't get updated systematically when passwords change.

What actually needs to happen

The gap isn't complicated to describe even if it's been difficult to solve. You need a way to store your important accounts and credentials in an organized, secure place. You need to be able to attach instructions to specific assets — not just "here's my password" but "here's what I want done with this account." You need a mechanism for verifying you're still alive so the information only releases when it actually needs to. And you need to designate specific people to receive specific things, not just one executor who gets everything at once.

The alive-check piece is actually the hardest problem. Because the whole system only works if it doesn't release your information prematurely. That requires some kind of regular verification — are you still there? — with a well-designed process for what happens if you don't respond.

There's also a trust architecture problem. If your executor is the only person who knows where everything is, you've created a single point of failure. If multiple people know, you've potentially created a security exposure. Any good solution has to thread that needle carefully.

The conversation most families don't have

Here's the practical takeaway from all of this.

The conversation you need to have isn't just "do you have a will." It's also: does someone trusted know what accounts exist? Does someone trusted know how to access them? Is there a written record of what you'd want done with your digital life? Is that record stored somewhere secure enough that it won't be accessed by the wrong person, but accessible enough that it won't be lost?

Most families haven't had that conversation. Not because they don't care, but because there hasn't been a clean way to make it happen.

In the meantime, the most important thing you can do right now is have the conversation. Not the full technical setup. Just the conversation. Tell someone you trust what exists, approximately where it is, and what you'd want them to do. It won't be perfect. But it's infinitely better than the alternative.

My uncle's family still doesn't have access to some of his accounts. Three years later. His photos are probably still there, sitting on a server somewhere, perfectly intact, completely out of reach. That outcome is entirely preventable. It just requires thinking about it before you need to.
